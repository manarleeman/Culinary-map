Afang Soup

A rich, nutritious vegetable soup made with afang leaves and waterleaf

About Afang Soup

Afang Soup is a traditional Nigerian delicacy that originated from the Efik-Ibibio people of Akwa Ibom and Cross River States in the southern part of Nigeria. This nutritious vegetable soup is prepared with a unique combination of afang leaves (Gnetum africanum) and waterleaf.

Known for its rich taste and high nutritional value, Afang Soup is typically prepared with various protein sources like meat, fish, and seafood. The unique flavor comes from the combination of afang leaves, which have a distinctive taste, and the umami richness provided by periwinkles, dried fish, and crayfish.

This soup holds significant cultural importance in southern Nigerian cuisine and is often served at ceremonies, celebrations, and special occasions. It pairs perfectly with fufu, pounded yam, garri (eba), or any other traditional Nigerian swallow.

Ingredients

Everything you need to prepare authentic Nigerian Afang Soup

Main Ingredients

  • 500g Afang leaves (Gnetum africanum), washed and sliced
  • 300g Waterleaf, washed and chopped
  • 500g Assorted meat (beef, goat meat, tripe)
  • 200g Dried fish, cleaned and deboned
  • 150g Periwinkles, cleaned
  • 100g Stockfish, soaked and cleaned
  • 200g Fresh or smoked fish
  • 1/2 cup Palm oil

For Seasoning

  • 3 tbsp Ground crayfish
  • 2 Seasoning cubes
  • 1 tsp Ground pepper (or to taste)
  • to taste Salt
  • 1 Medium onion, chopped

For Serving

  • Pounded yam, fufu, or garri (eba)
  • Fresh sliced pepper (optional)

üí° Ingredient Tips

  • If fresh afang leaves are not available, dried or frozen afang leaves can be substituted. Soak dried leaves in warm water before using.
  • Spinach can be used as a substitute for waterleaf if it's not available.
  • Ensure the periwinkles are properly cleaned to remove sand and grit.
  • Soak stockfish overnight before cooking to soften it properly.

üîÑ Substitutions

  • Fresh okazi leaves can be used instead of afang leaves.
  • Vegetarians can omit the meat and seafood and increase the quantity of mushrooms for umami flavor.
  • Snails can be used in place of or alongside periwinkles.

Preparation Method

Step-by-step guide to perfect Nigerian Afang Soup

1

Prepare the Proteins

Wash and season the assorted meat with onions, salt, and seasoning cube. Cook until tender, adding water as needed. Add the cleaned stockfish toward the end of cooking the meat.

Keep the meat stock (broth) as it adds flavor to the soup.
2

Prepare the Seafood

In a separate pot, boil the periwinkles for about 10 minutes. Clean and debone the dried fish, then add it to the pot with the periwinkles. Cook for another 5 minutes.

Periwinkles can be quite tough if not properly cooked.
3

Prepare the Vegetables

Wash the afang leaves and waterleaf thoroughly. Slice the afang leaves finely and chop the waterleaf. For dried afang leaves, soak in warm water for about 15 minutes, then drain before slicing.

Afang leaves can be tough, so ensure they are sliced very finely.
4

Start the Soup Base

In a large pot, heat palm oil on medium heat. Add the chopped onions and saut√© until translucent. Add the cooked meat, stockfish, and their stock. Bring to a boil.

5

Add Seafood and Seasonings

Add the periwinkles and dried fish to the pot. Stir in the ground crayfish, seasoning cubes, and ground pepper. Adjust salt to taste. Allow to simmer for about 10 minutes.

The soup should have a rich, flavorful broth at this stage.
6

Add the Vegetables

First, add the waterleaf to the pot and stir. Let it cook for about 5 minutes until it wilts. Then add the sliced afang leaves, stirring well to combine with the soup. Cover and allow to simmer for 10-15 minutes on low heat.

Adding the waterleaf first helps soften the afang leaves better.
7

Check Consistency and Serve

Check that the soup has reached your desired consistency. Afang soup should be somewhat thick but still have some liquid. Add a little water if it's too thick, or allow to simmer longer if too watery. Taste and adjust seasonings if necessary.

Serve hot with pounded yam, fufu, garri (eba), or any swallow of your choice.

Serving Suggestions

How to enjoy your Afang Soup like a true Nigerian

Traditional Pairings

In Nigeria, Afang Soup is traditionally served with these accompaniments:

  • Pounded Yam: The smooth, elastic texture of pounded yam pairs perfectly with the rich flavors of afang soup.
  • Fufu: Made from cassava, fufu has a similar consistency to pounded yam and is equally popular.
  • Garri (Eba): A staple made from cassava flour, slightly granular in texture and excellent for soaking up the soup.
  • Semolina or Amala: Other swallow options that go well with afang soup.

Serving Traditions

Afang soup is served in various settings across Nigeria:

  • Family Meals: Often prepared for Sunday family gatherings or special occasions.
  • Ceremonies: A common dish at weddings, child naming ceremonies, and other cultural celebrations in southern Nigeria.
  • Restaurants: Featured prominently in Nigerian restaurants specializing in traditional cuisine.

Eating Etiquette

The traditional way to enjoy afang soup:

  • Wash hands thoroughly before eating, as it's traditionally eaten with hands.
  • Take a small portion of the swallow with your fingers, form a small ball with an indentation.
  • Use this to scoop some soup and enjoy the combination of flavors.
  • Alternatively, cutlery can be used for a more modern approach.

Cultural Significance

The story behind Afang Soup

Afang Soup has deep cultural roots among the Efik, Ibibio, and other ethnic groups in southeastern Nigeria. The soup gets its name from its main ingredient, the afang leaf (Gnetum africanum), which grows abundantly in the tropical rainforests of West and Central Africa.

Traditionally, gathering afang leaves was a communal activity, with women venturing into the forests to harvest these nutritious leaves. The preparation of the soup itself often became a social activity, with knowledge of the recipe passed down from mothers to daughters across generations.

In Akwa Ibom and Cross River States, afang soup is more than just food‚Äîit's a symbol of hospitality and celebration. It's commonly prepared for important guests and during ceremonies such as marriages, child dedications, and funeral ceremonies. The presence of afang soup at these gatherings signifies respect for tradition and cultural heritage.

The soup also has health significance in local traditions. The afang leaf is believed to have medicinal properties and is considered beneficial for blood production, digestion, and overall wellness. Many elderly people in these communities attribute their good health to the regular consumption of vegetable soups like afang.

Tips & Variations

Master the art of Afang Soup

Chef's Tips

  • The key to a good afang soup is balancing the vegetables‚Äîtoo much afang can make the soup bitter, while too much waterleaf can make it too slimy.
  • Pound or blend a small portion of the waterleaf to help thicken the soup without using artificial thickeners.
  • Add the palm oil toward the end of cooking for a more vibrant color and fresh palm oil flavor.
  • For an extra layer of flavor, roast the ground crayfish lightly before adding to the soup.
  • Allow the soup to simmer slowly rather than boiling rapidly to develop a deeper flavor profile.

Regional Variations

Efik-Style Afang

The Efik people tend to make their afang soup with more seafood, especially periwinkles and fresh fish, reflecting their coastal heritage.

Ibibio Variation

In some Ibibio preparations, cocoyam is pounded and added to thicken the soup, giving it a distinct consistency.

Calabar Special

In Calabar, the soup often includes more exotic proteins like snails and bush meat, with extra spices for a more complex flavor profile.

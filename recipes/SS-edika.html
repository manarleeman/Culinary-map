Edikang Ikong Soup

A scrumptious vegetable soup from Calabar, Southern Nigeria, featuring an assortment of vegetables, palm oil, and spices

About Edikang Ikong Soup

Edikang Ikong is a nutritious vegetable soup from Calabar, Southern Nigeria. Made with a generous combination of pumpkin leaves, waterleaves, and a variety of proteins, this soup is truly a nation's favorite.

Packed with vitamins and minerals from the leafy vegetables and enriched with seafood and meat, Edikang Ikong offers a perfect balance of flavor and nutrition that represents the culinary excellence of South-South Nigerian cuisine.

Often referred to as the "Queen of Soups" due to its rich nutritional content and complex flavors, this dish has transcended its regional origins to become one of Nigeria's most celebrated dishes.

South South Traditional Efik-Ibibio Main Dish Soup

Ingredients

Everything you need to prepare authentic Edikang Ikong Soup

For Edikang Ikong Soup

  • 400g Ugu/pumpkin leaves, washed and sliced
  • 200g Waterleaves (or Gure in Yoruba), washed and sliced
  • 1kg Boiled assorted meat
  • 200g Periwinkle, cleaned
  • 200g Boiled Dried Stock Fish (pre-soaked)
  • 4 pieces Boiled Crabs and/or Snails
  • 100g Shrimps, cleaned
  • 50g Crayfish, ground
  • 300ml Palm oil
  • 1 large Onion, sliced
  • 1 large Fresh tomato, sliced
  • 2-3 pieces Red chilli peppers
  • 10g Locust beans
  • 3 Maggi cubes
  • To taste Salt

For Serving

  • 1 tuber Yam (optional)
  • As desired Chopped garlic (optional)

üí° Ingredient Tips

  • For the best flavor, use fresh vegetables rather than frozen ones.
  • Palm oil gives the soup its characteristic flavor and color. Don't substitute with other oils for an authentic taste.
  • Pre-soak the dried stock fish for at least 4 hours or overnight for best results.
  • The blend of different proteins makes this soup special - try to include as many as possible.

üîÑ Substitutions

  • If you can't find waterleaves, you can substitute with spinach, though the taste will be slightly different.
  • If periwinkle is unavailable, you can increase the quantity of other seafood.
  • For a less rich version, reduce the variety of meats and seafood.
  • Ground crayfish provides a unique flavor, but fish sauce can work as a substitute in a pinch.

Preparation Method

Step-by-step guide to perfect Edikang Ikong Soup

1

Heat the Palm Oil

Heat 3-4 cooking spoons of palm oil in a deep cooking pan, then add sliced onions.

Don't overheat the palm oil as it can become bitter. Heat it just enough to be hot but not smoking.
2

Add the Peppers and Tomatoes

Add blended pepper and allow to cook for 3 minutes. Add sliced red chilli peppers, tomatoes to add color and a little bit of chopped garlic (optional), then steam for 3 minutes.

This forms the flavor base for your soup. The garlic is optional but adds a pleasant depth of flavor.
3

Add Waterleaves

Add the sliced waterleaves and cook for 3 minutes. Do not add water because waterleaves generate water on their own.

Waterleaves release liquid when heated, so additional water isn't necessary at this stage.
4

Add Proteins and Seasonings

Add remaining ingredients: periwinkle, shrimps, boiled stock fish, smoked stock fish, boiled assorted beef, crayfish, crabs, snails, and locust beans. Season with Maggi cubes and salt. Stir all the ingredients together, then leave for 2 minutes.

This step combines all the protein elements that give Edikang Ikong its rich flavor profile.
5

Add Pumpkin Leaves

Add the Ugu (pumpkin leaves) and a bit of water, then stir and leave for 2 minutes.

Pumpkin leaves are added last to preserve their nutrients and vibrant green color.
6

Final Check and Serve

Taste and adjust seasoning if necessary. The soup should be thick but not too thick. Your delicious Edikang Ikong soup is ready to be served.

The soup is traditionally very rich in protein; feel free to adjust the quantities of meat and seafood according to your preference.

Serving Suggestions

How to enjoy your Edikang Ikong Soup

Traditional Pairings

Edikang Ikong Soup is traditionally served with:

  • Pounded Yam: A smooth, stretchy swallow made from boiled and pounded yam.
  • Fufu: Made from fermented cassava, often considered the perfect accompaniment.
  • Garri (Eba): A popular swallow made from processed cassava.
  • Semolina or Wheat: Modern alternatives that pair well with the soup.

Eating Technique

The traditional way to enjoy this meal:

  • With Hands: Take a small piece of the swallow, form a depression, and use it to scoop the soup.
  • Protein First: Many people eat the assorted meat and fish first before focusing on the swallow.
  • Balance: Ensure each scoop has both vegetables and some protein for the full flavor experience.

Serving Occasions

Edikang Ikong Soup is particularly associated with:

  • Special Occasions: Weddings, naming ceremonies, and important family gatherings.
  • Sunday Meals: Often prepared as a special weekend treat in many households.
  • Cultural Festivals: Featured in cultural celebrations in Cross River and Akwa Ibom states.
  • Restaurants: A premium dish in Nigerian restaurants, often at a higher price point due to its rich ingredients.

Cultural Significance

The story behind Edikang Ikong Soup

Edikang Ikong originated from the Efik-Ibibio people of Cross River and Akwa Ibom States in South-South Nigeria. Its name comes from the two main vegetable ingredients: "Edikang" (pumpkin leaves) and "Ikong" (waterleaves).

This soup holds a special place in Nigerian cuisine and is often referred to as the "Queen of Soups" due to its rich nutritional content and complex flavors. It's traditionally served at special occasions such as weddings, naming ceremonies, and important family gatherings.

In Efik-Ibibio culture, the skill of preparing Edikang Ikong was considered an essential attribute for young women, reflecting their ability to nourish their families with healthy and delicious food. The soup's preparation was often passed down from mother to daughter as part of cultural heritage.

Beyond its culinary importance, Edikang Ikong also represents the agricultural wealth of the South-South region. The abundance of fresh vegetables, seafood, and proteins in the soup highlights the fertility of the land and the proximity to rivers and the Atlantic Ocean.

"A pot of Edikang Ikong is like a canvas that tells the story of our land - the rivers, the forests, and the fertile soil that sustains us." ‚Äî Efik Saying

Tips & Variations

Master the art of Edikang Ikong Soup

Chef's Tips

  • For the best flavor, use fresh vegetables rather than frozen ones.
  • Don't overcook the vegetables to preserve their nutrients and vibrant color.
  • The soup should be thick but not too thick. Add a little water if necessary.
  • Try using three cubes of Maggi and a pinch of salt to ensure your cooking never has too much or too little salt.
  • Always taste before adding more salt to avoid over-seasoning.
  • Palm oil gives the soup its characteristic flavor and color. Don't substitute with other oils for an authentic taste.

Regional Variations

Calabar Style

Traditional version with more seafood like periwinkles and larger amounts of palm oil.

Akwa Ibom Variation

Often includes more crayfish and might include atama leaves for additional flavor.

Modern Adaptations

Some contemporary versions reduce the palm oil content and add bouillon cubes for enhanced flavor, catering to health-conscious diners.

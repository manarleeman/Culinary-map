Amala and Ewedu

A classic Yoruba delicacy featuring smooth yam flour swallow and mucilaginous jute leaf soup

About Amala and Ewedu

Amala and Ewedu is a quintessential Yoruba meal from Southwest Nigeria that has gained popularity throughout the country and West Africa. Amala is a smooth, stretchy swallow made from dried yam flour (elubo), while Ewedu is a mucilaginous soup prepared from jute leaves (Corchorus olitorius).

This nutritious combination is often served with gbegiri (bean soup) and assorted meat or fish to create what's lovingly called "abula" - a trio of soups that perfectly complement each other. Amala's deep brown color comes from the natural fermentation process of the yam before it's dried and ground into flour.

The pairing is a staple in Yoruba homes and restaurants, especially in Oyo State where it's considered the signature dish. Together, they offer a nutrient-rich meal with a unique taste and texture that represents the culinary heritage of the Yoruba people.

Ingredients

Everything you need to prepare authentic Amala and Ewedu

For Amala

  • 2 cups Yam flour (elubo)
  • 3-3.5 cups Water

For Ewedu Soup

  • 2 cups Fresh jute leaves (ewedu), picked and washed
  • 1 tbsp Potash (kaun) - optional
  • 2 tbsp Locust beans (iru)
  • 1 Small onion, diced
  • 1-2 Scotch bonnet peppers (to taste)
  • 2 cubes Bouillon/stock cubes
  • 1 tbsp Ground crayfish
  • 1 tsp Salt (to taste)
  • 2 cups Meat or fish stock

Optional Accompaniments

  • 1 cup Gbegiri (bean soup)
  • As desired Assorted meat or fish

üí° Ingredient Tips

  • Use high-quality yam flour for the best texture and taste.
  • Fresh jute leaves are ideal, but frozen ewedu leaves can work in their absence.
  • Potash helps achieve the slimy texture of ewedu but can be omitted if unavailable.
  • Traditional ewedu is made with an ewedu broom (ijabe) for proper consistency.

üîÑ Substitutions

  • If yam flour is unavailable, plantain flour can make a similar swallow.
  • Dried ewedu leaves can be rehydrated as a substitute for fresh ones.
  • Okra can be added to ewedu to enhance the slimy texture if potash is unavailable.
  • For a meatless version, use mushroom or vegetable stock.

Preparation Method

Step-by-step guide to perfect Amala and Ewedu

1

Prepare Ewedu Leaves

Pick through the ewedu leaves, removing stems and damaged parts. Wash thoroughly in cold water to remove any dirt or sand.

Fresh leaves give the best flavor, but ensure they're free of dirt which can make the soup gritty.
2

Cook Ewedu Base

In a medium pot, bring the meat or fish stock to a boil. Add the diced onions, scotch bonnet peppers, and locust beans. Simmer for 5 minutes.

The stock provides a savory base for the soup. If using potash, dissolve a small amount in water before adding.
3

Cook the Ewedu Leaves

Add the washed ewedu leaves to the simmering stock. Cook for 7-10 minutes until the leaves soften and begin to break down.

The leaves will reduce significantly in volume as they cook down.
4

Blend/Whisk the Ewedu

Using a traditional ewedu broom (ijabe) or hand blender, whisk the soup vigorously until the leaves break down and the soup becomes slimy and smooth. Alternatively, pulse briefly in a blender.

Don't over-blend! Ewedu should retain some texture while being smooth and mucilaginous.
5

Season the Ewedu

Return the blended ewedu to the pot if necessary. Add ground crayfish, bouillon cubes, and salt to taste. Simmer for another 5 minutes, stirring occasionally.

The soup should have a slightly viscous, slimy consistency when properly done.
6

Prepare for Amala

In a separate pot, bring 3-3.5 cups of water to a boil. Have your yam flour ready in a bowl.

The water-to-flour ratio determines the consistency of your amala. Adjust according to preference.
7

Make the Amala

Once the water is boiling, reduce heat to medium-low. Gradually add the yam flour, stirring continuously with a wooden spatula to prevent lumps.

Add the flour slowly while stirring to avoid lumps. The mixture will begin to thicken quickly.
8

Knead the Amala

Continue stirring and turning the amala, pressing out any lumps with your wooden spatula. Cover and let it cook on low heat for 2-3 minutes. Stir again until smooth and stretchy.

Properly cooked amala should be smooth, stretchy, and pull away from the sides of the pot.
9

Serve

Mold the amala into serving portions while hot. Serve with the ewedu soup, gbegiri (if using), and your choice of assorted meat or fish.

Traditionally served by placing the amala in the center of a plate and surrounding it with ewedu and gbegiri, creating a visually appealing presentation.

Serving Suggestions

How to enjoy your Amala and Ewedu like a true Yoruba

Traditional Pairings

Amala and Ewedu is traditionally served with:

  • Gbegiri: A smooth bean soup that adds creaminess to balance the sliminess of ewedu.
  • Assorted Meat: Beef, tripe, cowskin (ponmo), and offal are common protein options.
  • Stew: Sometimes a tomato-based stew is added for more flavor complexity.
  • Fish: Fried or smoked fish can be served alongside or within the ewedu.

Eating Technique

The traditional way to enjoy this meal:

  • With Hands: Pinch a small portion of amala with your right hand, form a small depression in it with your thumb.
  • Dipping: Dip the depression into the ewedu soup, then into gbegiri if available.
  • "Abula" Style: Some prefer to mix all components (ewedu, gbegiri, and stew) together and dip the amala in this mixture.
  • With Meat: Alternate between the soups and pieces of meat or fish for a complete dining experience.

Serving Occasions

Amala and Ewedu is particularly associated with:

  • Everyday Meals: A common lunch or dinner in Yoruba households.
  • Restaurants: A staple in "bukaterias" (local restaurants) across Southwest Nigeria.
  • Celebrations: Often served at weddings, naming ceremonies, and other cultural events.
  • Sunday Lunches: A popular choice for family gatherings after church services.

Cultural Significance

The story behind Amala and Ewedu

Amala and Ewedu represents more than just a meal in Yoruba cuisine‚Äîit embodies cultural identity and heritage. Originating from Oyo State, particularly Ibadan, this combination has become a culinary emblem of the Yoruba people.

The preparation of amala from yam flour showcases the ingenious food preservation techniques developed by the Yoruba. Yams, harvested seasonally, are processed into flour to ensure year-round availability‚Äîreflecting their agricultural wisdom and resourcefulness.

Ewedu, made from jute leaves, demonstrates the Yoruba people's knowledge of local vegetation and their ability to transform simple ingredients into nutritious meals. The plant itself has been cultivated for thousands of years, originally valued for its fiber before becoming a dietary staple.

The communal aspect of enjoying amala is significant in Yoruba culture. Traditionally, family members would sit around a single large plate, each person eating from their section‚Äîsymbolizing unity and the shared experience of nourishment. The act of eating with hands rather than utensils maintains a physical connection to the food and reinforces cultural traditions.

"Amala is not just food; it is an expression of our identity. The way it is prepared, served, and eaten tells the story of who we are as a people." ‚Äî Yoruba Proverb

Tips & Variations

Master the art of Amala and Ewedu

Chef's Tips

  • For smoother amala, sift the yam flour before cooking to remove any lumps or impurities.
  • Keep stirring the amala continuously until it's well-cooked to avoid lumps.
  • Add a small piece of smoked fish to your ewedu for an authentic depth of flavor.
  • The ewedu broom (ijabe) creates the perfect texture - a wire whisk can be a modern substitute.
  • Don't overcook ewedu leaves as they can lose their slimy properties and nutritional value.

Regional Variations

Ibadan Style

Known for serving "abula" - the perfect trio of ewedu, gbegiri, and amala with generous portions of assorted meat.

Ijebu Variation

Often includes more locust beans in the ewedu and prefers a slightly firmer amala texture.

Modern Adaptations

Some contemporary versions use unfermented yam flour for a lighter color or blend plantain flour with yam flour for a different flavor profile.
